    📊 The Trap of Probability and the Psychology of "Lucky" Stores

    We often forget the mathematical concept of "independent events". Just because the number 1 appeared last week doesn't mean the probability of it appearing this week decreases. Even if you flip a coin and get heads 10 times in a row, the probability of getting heads on the 11th flip remains exactly 50%.

    However, if we consider the 'Law of Large Numbers', the narrative shifts slightly. As the number of trials approaches infinity, the frequency of all numbers appearing should eventually equalize. This is why many analysts adopt the strategy of betting on 'Cold Numbers'—numbers that haven't been drawn in a long time.

    The Truth Behind "Lucky" Lottery Stores

    The secret behind so-called "lucky" stores that produce an unusually high number of jackpot winners is rooted in simple statistics: overwhelmingly high sales volume. If a store sells 10 times more tickets than an average store, the statistical probability that a winning combination exists within those tickets is naturally 10 times higher. What matters far more than superstitious behaviors—like traveling to a "lucky" store—is designing a rational number combination.

    🧠 Decision Fatigue Through the Lens of Neuroscience

    Modern humans make approximately 35,000 decisions every single day. From the moment we wake up to choosing a lunch menu and planning post-work schedules, we are caught in an endless loop of choices. With every decision, our prefrontal cortex burns through energy rapidly, leading to a state of cognitive overload known as 'Decision Fatigue'.

    MBTI Cognitive Functions and Consumer Behavior

    Interestingly, human preferences show distinct biases depending on one's innate personality typology (MBTI). For instance, types with dominant Si (Introverted Sensing) prefer "proven" restaurants that minimize the risk of disappointment, whereas types with high Ne (Extraverted Intuition) are strongly drawn to unfamiliar fusion cuisines and novel experiences.

    🃏 The Mirror of the Subconscious: Tarot and Synchronicity

    Tarot is not just a superstitious or unfounded fortune-telling tool. Prominent analytical psychologist Carl Jung evaluated Tarot as a powerful visual symbolic tool that brings the subconscious to the surface of consciousness. At its foundation lies the principle of 'Synchronicity', a phenomenon where two causally unrelated events are connected in a meaningful way.

    Among the 78 Tarot cards, the 22 Major Arcana cards—which form the core philosophical framework—depict the grand journey of life like an epic poem, starting from '0. The Fool' and concluding at '21. The World'.

    The single card you casually flip today may be the product of an algorithm's randomness. However, viewing the illustrations and symbols on that card and the fleeting 'interpretation' that your brain creates on its own is the true, honest message sent from your inner self.

    ⏱️ Cognitive Science: Defending Against the Brain's 'Aging Curve'

    Human visual reaction time is not simply a matter of how fast your finger muscles can twitch. It is a powerful indicator showing the health of a complex 'neural highway'—from the moment the color on the screen changes (visual stimulus), traveling through the optic nerve to the visual cortex, passing to the prefrontal cortex, and finally reaching the finger muscles via the spinal cord to execute a motor command.

    The Miracle of Neuroplasticity

    Human cognitive response peaks in the early 20s and gradually declines thereafter, experiencing what is known as an 'aging curve'. However, our brains are not set in concrete. If you continuously train to control your body in response to visual information, you can significantly delay aging through 'neuroplasticity'—the process by which the networks between synapses become more densely interconnected.
